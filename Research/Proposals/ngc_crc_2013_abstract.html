RADMAP: Runtime Analysis of Emerging Attack and System Health Data for Mission Planning and Execution

Saurabh Bagchi, Eugene Spafford

Funded by Northrop Grumman

Starting: September 2013

The armed forces routinely use distributed systems and applications running on them for purposes of mission planning and execution. During these stages, the states of the various components of the application and the machines that comprise the distributed system change, due to a variety of reasons - two primary factors are cyber attacks going on during mission planning and execution, and changes in the workload running on the computational infrastructure. We may have to determine quickly if the statically determined mission plan needs to be changed due to these changes. In the most challenging scenario, the determination has to be made while the mission is being executed. Therefore, it is natural to aim for an automated system that ingests information about attacks and the health of the various system components and outputs a mission plan and execution strategy that maximizes the likelihood of mission success. Thus, in this project, we are aiming for enhanced situational awareness, where the enhanced part comes in due to the following three main challenges to accomplishing this:

  • The data from sensors embedded in the distributed system is incomplete and only partially accurate.
  • The determination has to be done very quickly, though the infrastructure is distributed and the amount of data can be quite large (consider for example, that it is not uncommon for syslog entries and IDS entries to be generated in an active machine at > 1 MB/s).
  • The processing has to be done on streaming data, rather than data at rest.
We are also looking at fusing operational and intelligence information to dynamically update the mission plan and to maximize the probability of success of the mission, while being subjected to the “fog of war”. As has been defined in the cyber vision of all the branches of the US Department of Defense [1-3], cyber capabilities are no longer being viewed as only supporting kinetic capabilities, but also as a primary avenue for prosecuting war. Therefore, this calls into need a more scientific, adaptive, and robust mechanism for gathering evidence about the state of health of the cyber capabilities and tying that to the probability of success of the battlefield mission.
