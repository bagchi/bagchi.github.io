The wide deployment of high-speed computer networks has made distributed systems ubiquitous in today’s connected world. They form the backbone of much of the information technology infrastructure of the world today. The infrastructure, however, is increasingly facing the challenge of dependability outages resulting from both naturally occurring failures and malicious attacks, collectively referred to as disruptions in the proposal. The potential causes of downtime are manifold – hardware failures, system or application software failures, operator errors, maintenance (such as, backups and software upgrades), environmental problems (such as, power outages and communication lines being down). The outages may also be caused by malicious intruders launching attacks against the infrastructure through sending viruses, worms, malformed network packets, etc. designed to exploit vulnerabilities in the hardware or software design of the systems. The consequences of downtime of distributed systems are loss of revenue ($6M/hour for financial companies in the Fortune 1000 list), and loss of lives in the applications in safety critical sectors, such as aircraft control. 

This motivates the need for building distributed systems that are capable of tolerating disruptions and providing highly available and correctly functioning services. The challenges to providing this are many. The machines on which the applications are hosted are heterogeneous in nature, the applications often run legacy code without the availability of their source code, the systems are of very large scales, and the systems have soft real-time guarantees. While it may be possible to devise very optimized and targeted solutions for individual distributed applications, such approaches are not very interesting from a research standpoint due to their limited applicability.

In our research, we propose a two-pronged approach to the problem. We propose a monitor-based solution where a monitor observes the external interactions of protocol participants, matches them against a rule base of rules specified in temporal logic, and flags disruptions in case of a mismatch. In order to tolerate the latency of this approach, we propose a Reliability and Security Engine (RSE) which can be embedded in the target node to make it resistant to a defined class of disruptions.

The main contributions the project will make are:

1.      It will provide a hierarchical monitor based infrastructure that is scalable to very large distributed applications and applicable to a large class of applications. The monitor hierarchy can be structured according to the communication pattern and rule base for scalability. It will provide fast matching algorithms that can be customized according to the tolerable false alarm and missed alarm probabilities.

2.      It will provide a concise temporal rule base of legitimate behavior that can handle the impreciseness and variations in real-world protocol interactions and tools to create the rule base in an automated manner.

3.      It will provide a reliability and security engine implemented in hardware that is embeddable in off-the-shelf nodes and that provides low latency detection.

4.      It will provide a flexible architecture of RSE and different detection modules that can be embedded into it for detecting different types of disruptions.
